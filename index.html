Learning MTG: Introduction

MTG stands for Magic: The Gathering. It is a trading card game, where each player brings a deck to the table and plays against an opponent. It can be very complicated to learn, but a very rewarding and strategic experience if you do learn to play.

The goal of the game:

Each player starts with 20 health. If your opponent's heath goes to 0, they lose. If all your opponent(s) lose the game, you win If a card would allow you to win by some other means, you win.

Mana

Mana is a resource you use to play cards. There are five different types.

In order, clockwise: White, Blue, Black, Red, and Green.
These colors are associated with, respectectively, the lands Plains, Islands, Swamps, Mountains, and Forests. Mana is produced by its respective land.

Playing Cards:

  • Playing lands. Playing lands is simple. You can play one land per turn, no more. If you don't have any lands to play, too bad. You don't get a land.

  • Playing spells. Anything that isn't a land is a spell. To play spells, you need to use mana.

    The amount of mana needed to play a spell is included in the upper right-hand corner. It is called the mana cost. It is made up of little symbols in circles. Either they looks like the mana symbols on lands, or they looks like a number in a grey circle. This is called generic mana.

    • Lands only produce mana of a color. However, most cards have generic mana in their cost too. For example, look at khenra charioteer:

There are three symbols in the top. One is red mana, one is green mana, and one is a sngle generic mana. Green mana means you need one green mana to play it. Red mana means you need one red mana to play it. Generic mana means you need one mana of any color to play it. So, to play khenra charioteer you would need one red mana, one green mana, and one mana of any color. To get mana, it's easy. You tap a land, and that land produces a mana of its color. Tapping a card means turning it sideways. This usually symbolizes it being used up for the turn. You can only tap things once per turn - if something's tapped, you can't tap it again. So, in conclusion, to play khenra charioteer, you would have to tap one mountain, one forest, and one land of any color. This is the same for every spell(aka every card that is not a land.) - the generic mana needs to be paired up with the colored mana to cast the spell. In this example. we used a creature, although the card types vary. Mana costs are different from card to card, which influences how you play any given card.

Important note: In our examples, the generic mana is represented by a 1 in a circle. However, this is often not the case. Any number can be in that gray circle. However, several colored mana symbols each require their own symbol. In the example of Ancient Silverback (shown below), he requires four generic mana, or mana of any color, and two green mana. Notice how the four is in a single gray circle, but both green mana are in separate circles.

Creatures

Creatures are one of the most important card types in magic, and also one of the more complicated, because they have to engage in combat. Take a look at a creature. We already saw this one - ancient silverback.

In the top left-hand corner of a creature, you have its name, and in the top right, its mana cost. These will be present regardless of whether or not this is a creature; they're in every card. Under that, a card's art, and below that, its type. In this case, since the silverback is a creature, it says "creature", and then specifies ape. Most card types do not contain this specification, but creatures do. There could be several things, like "goblin shaman" or "human warrior". In this case, however, it just says "ape". To the far right of that section is the set symbol. It isn't really important in terms of the game. It's color is different depending on how rare it is and the symbol is different depending on when it was released but this doesn't really matter for the game.

We're going to skip the center section to go down to the bottom for a second. On the bottom, you can see the artist's name on the left. The really important part, however, is the far right; The two numbers separated by a slash. These two numbers are a creature's power and toughness. These are the creature's attack power (power) and health (toughness). Important: attack (Power) is always on the left and health (toughness) is always on the right. Damage in MTG is different from damage in other games. Damage in MTG lasts until the end of the turn, then it goes away. That means that if you want to kill a creature, you have to do it in a single turn, or it'll just recover when the turn is over. Look again at our ape. He has toughness 5, which means that to kill him you need to do five damage in one turn. If a creature takes damage greater than or equal to its toughness (aka if it ever goes to 0 or lower health), it dies immediately. When a creature dies, it goes to the graveyard, which is MTG speak for discard pile. Power is the damage a creature does. This damage is the same whether the damage is to players or creatures. While we're not going to get into combat right now, know that a creature's power is its attack and its toughness is its health.

MTG player sometimes refer to a creature by using its power and toughness. For example, a MTG player might say about Ancient Silverback "It's a 6/5". This sounds like six-five and is a very commonly used sort of MTG slang.

Now lets go back up to the big square of text under the image. This is a creature's text box, and its the most complicated part of a creatures, and possibly the most important. It's what differentiates it from all of the other creatures in MTG history. There are a few different kinds of things that can go in a text box. First off, a keyword ability.

At the top of the text box, in shivan dragon, we see something that says flying. This is an example of a keyword ability. This means that it's an ability that recurs and can be found on many cards throughout magic. Keyword abilities also often have explanations of their what they do in italics behind the ability itself, like on shivan dragon. Khenra charioteer is another good example of a keyword ability (in this case, the ability is trample and there is no explanation behind it). If, in the text box, there's just one word set higher than the rest, it's usually a keyword ability. Note: a creature can have any number of keyword abilities, like flying and trample. There isn't a hard set limit.

Another thing khenra charioteer can teach us is about passive abilities. Under its keyword ability, khenra charioteer has a text that says "Other creatures you control have trample". This is a passive ability: it takes place all the time, regardless of whether you do something or not. Its effect is giving all of your creatures the keyword ability trample (what that ability does is not important right now). Most keyword abilities are passive abilities that just happen to be found on many cards.

Here's another type of ability. A triggered ability. This is a like a passive ability in the sense that you don't have to do anything to activate it, but is different in the sense that it isn't a continuous effect - rather, it happens everytime a certain thing happens (specified in the ability). Soul warden, for example, is a simple yet very good example of a triggered ability.

What it says is that whenever a creature enters the battlefield, you gain 1 life. You start at 20 life, and when your life goes to 0 you lose. Battlefield is MTG speak for playing field. This ability costs you nothing when it triggers, but only triggers sometimes. It is a triggered ability.

Activated Abilities:
Activated abilities are probably the most complex kind of ability in Magic: the Gathering. They work differently than the other abilities we talked about before in that they have a cost. In fact, we've already looked at cards with said activated abilities. Let's jump back to one: Shivan Dragon

Under the keyword ability flying, we see another ability. Activated abilities consist of two parts: a cost and an effect. It's easy to tell them apart: the cost is on the left side of the colon and the effect is on the right side. In this example, the cost of the ability is a single red mana, represented by the red mana symbol, and the effect is that Shivan Dragon gets +1/+0 until end of turn. What this means is that Shivan Dragon's power (attack) goes up by 1. However, his toughness (health) goes up by 0, so it remains unchanged. Hence, Shivan Dragon would have the stats 6/5 after using this ability once (and you would have spent one red mana). This bonus disappears at the end of your turn. Important Note: you can use an activated ability as many times per turn as you want (provided you have the mana to pay for it). So you could use Shivan Dragon's ability as much as you have the mana for in any given turn, making him a 7/5, 8/5, or even higher.

The last type of ability we will cover will be mana ability. This is esentially the same as an activated ability, with a cost and an effect, but this study will introduce several key concepts. Here is Elvish Mystic:

We can see something that looks like an activated abbility in Elvish Mystic's text box. In the cost, it has the symbol that looks like a sideways arrow. This symbol stands for tap. Here's a closer look.

Tap, if you remember, means turning a creature sideways. It usually means that that creature is "used up" for the turn. It is in Elvish Mystic's cost, which means that to use that ability you need to tap him. If a creature is already tapped, you can't tap it again. So, for this ability, Elvish Mystic needs to be untapped. Then you tap him, and the effect of his ability occurs.

Let's look more closely at this effect. It says add 1 green mana (remember, the tree is the symbol for green mana) to your mana pool. When you tap a land for mana, you don't have to spend it right away. It goes to somewhere called a mana pool, where you have mana of different colors waiting around for you to use them. This mana pool empties at different sections during your turn, so you mostly just tap lands for mana when you're going to cast something. So when Elvish Mystic uses his ability, you get 1 green mana, to spend on spells.

This example introduces some key concepts. First of all, that mana can come from sources other than lands. So, Elvish Mystic can produce mana, that you use to cast spells. There are some other cards that can do this as well. Second of all, the idea that tapping a creature is something in an ability cost. This actually isn't an uncommon thing; many cards require tapping to activate their abilities. And now let's get back to what a mana ability is. A mana ability is an ability that produces mana. It's basically an activated ability, but it produces mana. The only reason I included this section is because cards that reference activated abilities don't apply to mana abilities. They work almost exactly the same way, though.

The last part in a creature's text box we have to talk about is the flavor text. It doesn't apply at all to the game, but it can be cool to read. Almost all of the cards we talked about have had flavor text. It's in the way bottom of the card and in italics. Here's an example:

The text that says "We do not swerve - Tah-crop charioteer motto" is, in this case, the flavor text. All of the cards we have looked at so far have had flavor text, except for Ancient Silverback. Often, if a card doens't have flavor text it's because the rest, and more important part, of the text box takes too much space.

Notice, the large text box results in no space for flavor text.

Enchantments

Enchantments are another class of card in MTG, and they are, at their base, relatively simple. An enchantment is, like creatures and lands, a permanent. A permanent is a card that stays on the battlefield, instead of simply being cast and then going away. We'll see examples of card like that a few sections later. Anyhow, an enchantment is probably the simplest kind of permanent. Here is an example of a simple enchantment:

While greater auramancy, at first glance, looks a lot like a creature card, there are a few key differences. First, observe the bar between the image and the text box on the card. It says "enchantment". Just about every card has this card type indicator and it is very handy. Notice, however, that there is no specification. For normal enchantments, there is no specification after the word enchantment. Also note that an enchantment doesn't have power or toughness. Only creatures have those. Enchantments also almost never have Keyword abilities. They can, however, have all the other kinds of abilities. Enchantments also have flavor text, same as creatures.

Below is an example of an enchantment with a triggered ability, an activated ability, and a passive ability.

Kindred boon has an ability that activates when it enters the battlefield, an ability that you need to pay mana to activate, and a passive ability. Note: while it is possible to have a mana ability on an enchantment, and an ability that requires you to tap an enchantment, it is very rare.

So far enchantments seem pretty simple. Where things get complicated, however, is the two special types of enchantments: enchantment creatures and enchantment auras.

Enchantment Creatures:
Enchantment creatures are actually pretty simple: they work exactly like creatures, except they're also affected by things that affect enchantments. Say I had a card that allowed me to destroy an enchantment, but didn't specifically let me destroy a creature. I could still destroy an enchantment creature. However, if you have a card that gets stronger the more enchantments you have, enchantment creatures count.

A way to tell you're dealing with an enchantment creature is the glowy stardust effect around the border. Also, look where it normally says creature. It says "enchantment creature". That's to help you figure out what card type it is.
Side note specific to this example:
We can see that Nyx-Fleece Ram's power(attack) is 0. That's actually totally fine. A creature can have power 0, it just won't deal any damage. If a creature ever has toughness 0, though, it dies immediately.

Enchantment Auras:
Enchantment auras are the final and most complicated type of enchantment. They are so complicated because they attach to something. Let's take a look at one; Angelic Gift:

This card seems identical to a normal enchantment, in all but a few respects. First of all, where the card type (enchantment) is shown, there is a specification. We see it specifies "aura", which is the type of card we are talking about. Also, the first line of text says "Enchant Creature".
The crucial thing to understand about auras is that they attach to something. An aura can't be played on its own. If you play an aura and there isn't anything to attach it to it goes away immediately. Here we can see that it says "Enchant creature". What this means is that you would attach this aura to a creature

In practice, you often put the aura on top or under the creature card, but slightly offset (so you can still see the card underneath). This would help you remember that the aura applies to this particular creature. An aura always exerts an effect on a creature if it's attached to one. The creature it affects is called the "enchanted creature". In this case, this aura gives the enchantd creature +1/+1 and flying. What this means is that the creature's power and toughness stats would each be raised by one, as we have +1 for the power and +1 fpr the toughness. This aura would also apply the Keyword "flying" to the creature it is attached to. This aura also has a triggered ability that causes you to draw a card when you play it.

Auras can also affect players, not just creatures. Here is an example of an aura that affects a player:

When the creature, player, or other card an aura is affected leaves the battlefield (playing field) for any reason, the aura is immediately destroyed. It doesn't switch to another card, it's just destroyed.

It isn't attached to anything yet, but you can equip it to a creature later. You can also do this while the creature it's equipped to is still there - pay the mana and move the equipment from one creature to another.

In these ways, we've seen how equipments are at the same time similar, yet different, to auras.

Instants and Sorceries

Instants and Sorceries are different than the card types we've already seen in one main respect: they aren't permanents. So, they don't hang around after you play them. Basically, instants and sorceries have an effect, in their text box, that happens when they are played. After this effect, the instant or sorcery card goes into the graveyard. The graveyard is the MTG equivalent of a discard pile. Anyhow, the instant or sorcery has an effect, and then it goes to the graveyard (discard pile). At this point, you might be wondering why I keep referring to instants and sorceries as the same thing. This is because they are essentially identical, with one key difference, which we'll get to a little later. Here's a sorcery card:

Notice it looks pretty similar to an enchantment, no power or toughness, but a mana cost, name, text box, etc. Where the card type is specified, it says sorcery. In this case, this card's effect would be that you draw two card from your library (MTG speak for deck), and add it to your hand. If, now, we look at an instant, it's a little bit difficult to see the difference.

Obviously we can see that in the card type tag it says "instant", but for the most part the card looks the same. It has an effect that happens when it enters the field (dealing 2 damage to a creature or player), and then it goes in the graveyard. So, in the end, what is the difference between instants and sorceries?

The difference, it turns out, comes down to when you can play the card. During a turn, there's a set time when you can play stuff, called a main phase. We're going to explain the full turn later, but suffice it to say that the main phase is when you play stuff. You can only play sorceries when you play everything else, during the main phase. Instants, however, can be player whenever you want. They're a sort of "ace in the hole" that you can surprise your opponents with. So, that's the difference between instants and sorceries.

There is really only one subtype for instants and sorceries, and it's nowhere near as complicated as for artifacts and enchantments. This type is "tribal". Here is an example of a tribal instant (although sorceries can be tribal too).

This doesn't really change the game in any other way, though. Also, this is the only time that instants or sorceries have anything specified in their card type.

Lands

Lands are the backbone of any deck in MTG. They are exceedingly important to understand.You can play one land per turn, every turn, during the phase when you play everything else. First, let's talk about basic lands.

Basic lands are, for the most part, very simple. They come in five colors: red, green, blue, white, and black, each tied into ,respectively, mountain, forest, island, plains, and swamp. Thye each have a very simple ability: they tap and add one mana of the color they are to your mana pool. Here are the five basic lands:

The turn:

A turn in MTG has many phases. Here they are, in order.

  • The untap step.
    When you use cards, they becomed tapped, which means they are turned sideways, to represent that they have been used for the turn. During this step, you turn all your cards forward again, to show that they are ready to use again.
  • The Upkeep.
    Normally, nothing happens during this step. However, it's used to mark the start of a turn. For example, it a card wants something to happen at the start of your turn. it'll say "at the beginning of your upkeep", which means during this phase.
  • The Draw Phase.
    At the start of this phase, you draw a card from your library, and you put it in your hand. Library is MTG speak for deck.
  • First Main Phase.
    During this turn, you play cards from your hand. You can play one land per turn, and it is during your main phase that you play it. Just about every other card type can also only be played during your main phase. Instants, however, are the exception. You can play instants whenever you want, including during the upkeep or untap step, or even the attack step.
  • The Attack phase.
    The attack phase is a very important phase, as it is often during this phase that players take damage and creatures die. The attack phase follows a very specific order. Here are the steps:
    1. Declare attackers:
      The player whose turn it is declares how many creature he wants to attack with and which creatures he wants to attack. This player can choose to attack with no creatures, which means the rest of this phase does not happen. However, effects that happen during the attack phase still happen, so the phase is technically not skipped. Any creatures that are attacking tap, unless they have vigilance.
    2. Declare blockers:
      The player who is being attacked, the defending player, picks how many creatures he wants to block with, and which creatures he wants to block. He can block all of the creatures or none of the creatures, and each attacking creature can be blocked by as many creatures as the defending player wants. So, if the defending player gets attacked by three creatures, he can block one of the attackers with one creature, one of the attackers with four creatures, and let the other attacker go through for damage.
    3. Damage:
      Then, damage is dealt. Remember, creatures with first strike do damage before creatures without it. Any creatures not blocked do an amount of damage equal to their power(number on the left) to the defending player, reducing his health total by that many points. If there is one creature blocking one creature, what happens is simple. They both deal damage equal to their power to each other. If it is enough to kill one or both of them, those creatures die. If there are several creatures blocking a single creature, then the damage of all of the different creatures is added up against the lone one, but the lone creature's power must be spread out against all of the different creatures. Then, combat is over.
  • Second Main Phase.
    This phase is identical to the First Main Phase with the exception that it comes after, and not before, the attack step. Also, some cards refer to one main phase or another.
  • The End Step
    This is the final phase in a turn. Some cards have effects that say "at the beginning of your end step", which means they would happen at the end of your turn. All effects that say "until end of turn" end. All damage is removed from creatures. At this stage, if you have more than seven cards in your hand you must discard down to seven.

The turn and the mana pool.
After the end of each phase, all mana is emptied from your mana pool. This includes the end step and also the phases of other players' turns. This means that you should never tap more lands than you need to, because their mana for that turn would be wasted. This also means that if an effect causes you to gain a set amount of mana, you should make sure you have that ability to use said mana.

Playing the Game

Now that you know the rules, you're ready to play. But there are still some things to be worked out.

Setting up.
To play the game, there are a few things you need. First of all you need am opponent, or opponents. While there it technically no limit to how many people can play a single game of MTG, the more people you have the less time everyone has to play, and the more complicated the game becomes, so you probably shouldn't play with more than 8 or 9 people in a single game, and even that stretches it. Also, games with this many people easily take hours.
Once you have found someone to play with, you have to set up the playing field. Shuffle your library, or deck, and allow your opponent to cut before dealing yourself a hand of seven cards. Place your library beside you, with room for the battlefield and for your graveyard, MTG speak for discard pile.
Note: graveyards are not shared.
Find a way to count your life total, usually using pen and paper or dice, and set your life to 20. Determine who will go first, using some sort of random method, and then start to play.
Note: In a two player game, the first person to play does not draw a card on their first turn. In a multiplayer game, however, everyone draws on their first turn regardless of who goes first.
